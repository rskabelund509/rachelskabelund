Part One

The #1 Mistake Everyone Makes

Why does AI give me generic answers?

Here's the truth most people never realize: AI is a mirror. Vague questions get vague answers. Specific, context-rich questions get expert-level responses.

‚ùå What most students type
"What causes chest pain?"
‚úì What power users type
"I'm a second-year medical student studying for Step 1. Explain the differential diagnosis for acute chest pain in a 55-year-old male with diabetes and hypertension, organized by most likely etiology. Include the key distinguishing features for each."

The second prompt gives AI the context to tailor its response to your level, your purpose, and your specific scenario.

üí° The Rule: Treat AI like a brilliant colleague who just walked into the room. They're incredibly capable, but they don't know your situation yet. The more context you provide, the more useful they become.

Part Two

AI as Your Personal Tutor

Accelerate your learning ‚Äî don't skip it

Here's the mindset shift that separates students who thrive from those who struggle:

‚ùå Replacement mindset: "AI can write my notes and answer questions so I don't have to learn."

‚úì Acceleration mindset: "AI can explain things 15 different ways until one clicks, quiz me relentlessly, and help me build understanding faster than ever before."

AI is the most patient teacher you'll ever have. It will explain the Krebs cycle using a pizza delivery analogy if that's what helps. It will generate 50 practice questions at 2am without complaining. It will break down a complex paper paragraph by paragraph until you actually understand it.

Without AI With AI (Used Well)
Read the chapter, hope it sticks Read the chapter, then ask AI to quiz you and explain gaps
Re-read highlighted notes passively Ask AI to generate clinical vignettes from your notes
Struggle alone with confusing concepts Get instant, personalized explanations until it clicks
Passive review before exams Active retrieval practice on demand, any time

The key insight: AI doesn't learn for you ‚Äî it creates more opportunities for you to learn. Every explanation you request, every quiz you take, every "explain that differently" builds understanding that passive reading never will.

üí° Try This Today
"I just learned about [topic] but I'm shaky on it. First, quiz me with 5 questions to see what I actually know. Then explain the concepts I got wrong using simple analogies. Finally, give me 3 more questions to confirm I understand."

That single prompt creates a complete active learning cycle that would have taken hours to set up manually.

Part Three

Not All AI Is Created Equal

Understanding model differences

Model Tier Best For Think of it as...
GPT-4 / Claude Opus Complex reasoning, nuanced analysis, research synthesis Attending physician
GPT-4o / Claude Sonnet Balanced speed + intelligence, daily study use Senior resident
GPT-3.5 / Smaller models Quick facts, simple lookups, basic questions Medical student

Why this matters: Using a lightweight model for complex differential diagnosis is like asking a first-year to run a tumor board. Match the tool to the task.

Pro tip: If an AI gives you a shallow answer, it might not be the prompt ‚Äî it might be the model. Try a more capable one before rewriting your question.

Part Four

The Context Window

Your secret weapon for better responses

The "context window" is how much information AI can hold in memory during a conversation. Think of it as working memory ‚Äî the bigger it is, the more information AI can reference at once.

  • Small context window = AI forgets earlier parts of your conversation
  • Large context window = AI can reference an entire lecture, paper, or case study

How to use this strategically:

  1. Paste in source material ‚Äî Give the AI your lecture notes, a paper abstract, or detailed case information
  2. Keep important info near the end ‚Äî AI pays more attention to recent messages
  3. Start fresh for new topics ‚Äî Old context can confuse new questions
‚ö° Power Move
"Here are my notes from today's cardiology lecture: [paste notes]. Now quiz me on the key concepts with clinical vignette-style questions similar to Step 1."

You just created a personalized study tool that knows exactly what you learned today.

Part Five

Feed It Sources, Get Better Answers

AI doesn't know what it doesn't know

AI models have knowledge cutoffs and can "hallucinate" ‚Äî confidently stating incorrect information. The solution? Give it the source material.

‚ùå Instead of asking
"What's the latest treatment for HFrEF?"
‚úì Try this
"Based on the 2023 ACC/AHA heart failure guidelines (which I'll paste below), summarize the first-line treatments for HFrEF and explain the evidence level for each. [paste guideline excerpt]"

Now the AI is synthesizing real, current information ‚Äî not guessing from training data that might be outdated.

Sources to feed AI:

  • Lecture slides or transcripts
  • UpToDate summaries
  • Paper abstracts or full text
  • Clinical guidelines
  • Your own notes
Part Six

Always Verify

The trust but verify rule

AI is confident. That doesn't mean it's correct.

AI will never say "I don't know" with uncertainty in its voice. It delivers wrong answers with the same confidence as right ones. This is one of its most dangerous characteristics.

Your verification toolkit:

  • üîç Web search ‚Äî Sanity check facts and statistics
  • üìö UpToDate ‚Äî Gold standard for clinical questions
  • üìñ Primary sources ‚Äî PubMed, original guidelines
  • üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Faculty & attendings ‚Äî When stakes are high

The more consequential the information, the more important verification becomes. Studying for an exam? Verify key facts. Real patient care? Never rely on AI alone.

Part Seven

Your Agency Is Sacred

Don't outsource it to a machine

As students at BYU, you understand agency differently than most. It's not just about independence or autonomy ‚Äî it's a divine gift. The ability to act, to choose, to reason, and to bear responsibility for those choices is central to who you are and who you're becoming.

AI can't want things. It can't care about outcomes. It has no conscience, no accountability, and no growth. When you delegate your thinking entirely to a machine, you're not just being lazy ‚Äî you're stepping back from the very thing that makes you you.

"Use AI to inform your choices, never to make them for you."

Think of any task as three parts:

Stage Who Owns It Example
The Beginning
Defining the problem, setting direction		 YOU "What's actually going on with this patient?"
The Middle
Research, drafting, synthesizing options		 AI excels here "Generate a differential, summarize this paper"
The End
Final judgment, decisions, responsibility		 YOU "Here's what I think and why."

When you let AI handle the beginning and the end, you become a passive relay station. Information flows through you, but you never actually engage with it. This is how you end up unable to explain your own reasoning.

Remember: You will stand in front of patients, families, and colleagues who trust your judgment. AI can help you prepare for that moment. But when it arrives, the choice ‚Äî and the responsibility ‚Äî will always be yours.

The goal isn't to think less. It's to think better. AI serves that purpose. But the thinking? That's yours. Guard it.

Part Eight

Common Misconceptions

Myths that hold students back

Myth Reality
"AI will give me the right answer" AI gives you an answer. Correctness is your job to verify.
"Longer prompts are annoying" AI loves context. More detail = dramatically better responses.
"AI is cheating" AI is a tool, like a calculator. Using it wisely is a skill worth developing.
"I can just copy AI responses" AI is a drafting partner. Your judgment must refine the output.
"All AI chatbots are basically the same" Models vary wildly in capability. Choosing the right tool matters.
"AI knows the latest research" AI has knowledge cutoffs. For recent info, provide sources yourself.
